During a power outage, keep refrigerator and freezer doors closed as much as possible. A closed refrigerator can usually keep food cold for several hours, and a full freezer stays cold longer than a half-empty freezer. Opening the door repeatedly lets cold air escape quickly.

When deciding what to eat first, use perishable refrigerated foods before shelf-stable foods. Prioritize meat, fish, dairy, cooked leftovers, cut fruit, and prepared foods while they are still safely cold. Shelf-stable foods such as sealed cans, dry grains, crackers, peanut butter, and unopened shelf-stable milk can be saved for later.

Discard perishable food that has been warm for too long, smells wrong, feels slimy, has visible mold, or has leaked from damaged packaging. Do not taste questionable food to test it. Food poisoning can become dangerous when medical help, clean water, or transport are limited.

If you have ice, a cooler, or frozen water bottles, group high-risk foods together and keep them cold. Melted ice water can contaminate food if packaging leaks, so keep food sealed and drain dirty water away from eating surfaces.

Cook food thoroughly when fuel is available, but cooking does not make spoiled food safe. When in doubt, choose lower-risk shelf-stable food and conserve fuel for water treatment, warmth, or medically important needs.
