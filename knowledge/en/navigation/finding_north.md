If you need direction during a disaster, evacuation, or infrastructure failure and do not have a compass, use more than one method when possible. Treat improvised direction finding as approximate, then confirm with landmarks, maps, roads, stars, sunrise, sunset, or repeated checks.

Shadow stick method: place a straight stick upright in open sunlight. Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone. Wait 15 to 30 minutes, then mark the new shadow tip. In the northern hemisphere, a line from the first mark to the second mark points roughly west to east. Stand with west on your left and east on your right; you are facing roughly north.

Analog watch method: in the northern hemisphere, hold an analog watch level and point the hour hand at the sun. South is roughly halfway between the hour hand and 12 o'clock. North is the opposite direction. Adjust mentally for daylight saving time by using 1 o'clock instead of 12 o'clock.

Night method: in the northern hemisphere, find the Big Dipper if visible. The two outer stars of its bowl point toward Polaris, the North Star. Polaris sits roughly above true north. If clouds, buildings, smoke, or light pollution block the sky, do not rely on this method.

Improvised needle compass: magnetize a sewing needle by rubbing it in one direction against a magnet, or carefully against silk, wool, or hair many times. Place the needle on a small leaf, cork, or paper and float it in still water away from metal, phones, speakers, batteries, and wires. When it settles, it should align north-south, but it may not show which end is north. Compare it with sunrise, sunset, stars, or a known road before trusting it.

Avoid making travel decisions from one weak clue. Metal objects, electrical equipment, moving water, wind, slopes, and poor visibility can mislead improvised methods. If direction is uncertain and staying put is safe, conserve energy, mark your location, and signal for help.
