For minor cuts and scrapes, first stop bleeding with gentle direct pressure. Wash your hands if possible, then rinse dirt from the wound with clean running water or the safest available water. Remove loose debris with clean tweezers if available.

Do not scrub deep wounds aggressively, pour harsh chemicals into tissue, or close a dirty wound tightly. Cover the wound with clean gauze, cloth, or a sterile dressing. Change the dressing when it becomes wet, dirty, or loose.

Watch for infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, worsening pain, pus, red streaks, fever, or a bad smell. Infection risk is higher with animal bites, human bites, puncture wounds, dirty wounds, burns, diabetes, poor circulation, or weakened immunity.

Seek medical care urgently for deep wounds, uncontrolled bleeding, numbness, loss of movement, wounds over joints, bites, embedded objects, dirty punctures, or signs of infection. Tetanus prevention may be needed for dirty wounds when medical care becomes available.

Keep wounds dry and protected from floodwater, sewage, dirt, and shared towels. If supplies are limited, prioritize clean coverage for wounds that are bleeding, open, dirty, or likely to rub against clothing.
