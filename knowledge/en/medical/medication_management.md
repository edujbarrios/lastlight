During a blackout, gather essential medicines, written prescriptions, dosing instructions, medical devices, chargers, and health information in one place. Keep medicines dry, shaded, and away from children. Do not remove labels unless privacy or safety requires it.

Prioritize medicines that prevent immediate harm, such as insulin, seizure medicine, heart medicine, asthma inhalers, blood pressure medicine, anticoagulants, and psychiatric medicines. Do not intentionally skip critical medicine to stretch supply unless a qualified clinician tells you to.

Some medicines need refrigeration. Keep them in the coolest safe place available, such as an insulated bag with cool packs, but avoid freezing unless the label says freezing is safe. If temperature-sensitive medicine may have been damaged, keep it labeled and ask a pharmacist or clinician as soon as possible.

Track doses on paper when routines are disrupted. Write the date, time, medicine name, and dose after taking it. This helps prevent missed doses or accidental double doses when people are tired, stressed, or caring for others.

If evacuation is possible, carry medicines with you rather than leaving them behind. If supply is running low, contact pharmacies, clinics, emergency services, shelters, or neighbors early, before the last dose is gone.
