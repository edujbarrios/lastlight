Shock can happen after severe bleeding, burns, infection, dehydration, allergic reaction, or major injury. Warning signs include pale or clammy skin, weakness, rapid breathing, fast pulse, confusion, dizziness, thirst, nausea, or loss of consciousness.

Call for emergency help when possible. Keep the person still, warm, and lying down if that does not worsen breathing or injury. Control visible bleeding with steady direct pressure. Do not give food or drink if surgery, vomiting, severe injury, or reduced alertness is possible.

If the person is not breathing normally, follow local emergency instructions for CPR if you know how. If they vomit or become less responsive, protect the airway and watch breathing closely.

Shock is not solved by reassurance alone. Treat the likely cause, keep the person warm, and seek medical care as soon as conditions allow.
